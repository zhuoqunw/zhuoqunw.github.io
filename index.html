Behind the medal table: What matters to a country's success in winter Olympics?

Lea Wei (zhuoqunw@umich.edu)

Norway stunned the world with its success in Pyeongchang 2018 winter Olympics. The tiny country with about 5 million people dominated the medal tally and set a new record for medals with time to spare. As shown in the medal tally below, Norway sits first in the total number of medals. It also has won more gold, silver, and bronze models than any other country in the world.

(Data source: New York Times)

However, this was not the first time that Norway stood out on the playing field of winter Olympic Games. If we take all medals in history into account, we will find Norway, again, sits top in all kinds of medals. Moreover, from the Interactive map below, we can also obtain some interesting insights into the geographical distribution of medals. Since darker color in the color spectrum represents for more medals, apparently, countries in North America and Northern Europe, have better performance in winter Olympics in terms of the number of medals than other parts of the world. What is the magic of these countries? Or, more specifically, to be successful in winter Olympic Games, what matters and what doesn’t matter?

(Data source: Statista)

What matters: wealth, development, income inequality or national power?

There’s no shortage of discussion of what makes a country successful in winter Olympics. Popular reasons include more power and strength, lower income inequality, richer (i.e. higher GDP per capita), more developed and more star athletes, to name a few. Below, we will take a closer look at each possible reason.

(Data source: World Bank, UNDP)

As clearly shown in the scatter plots above, there is a positive relationship between GDP per capita and the number of medals, meaning the richer the country is, the better its performance in the winter Olympics. Note that here, we use GDP per capita instead of GDP to evaluate whether the country is rich or not because we care more about economic well-being on the individual level. A country with a larger GDP only means it has more economic power, but it doesn't necessarily mean that people living in that country are richer. Similarly, number of medals is also positively related to the Human Development Index, indicating that more developed countries usually have more medals in the Olympics.

However, it seems that there is no relationship between income inequality and the number of medals. As we can see in the plot, among top countries in the winter Olympics, Norway and Germany have relatively lower Gini coefficient, yet the U.S. and Russia have relatively higher Gini coefficient. In addition, some countries with very low Gini index, such as Hungary and Poland, only have won very few medals. Similarly, more power and strength do not equal success either. China has more popluation and GDP than most of its competitors in Pyeongchang, however, it didn't win more medals than countries whose GDP and population are way smaller, such as Norway, Germany, and Canada.

Top athletes are also important to a country's success in winter Olympics

Top athletes that can win multiple medals in a game are also important to a country's success. From the visualization below, and recall our medal tally, it is not hard to conclude that top countries also have top athletes. Take Norway as an example. Among top 10 athletes that have won most medals in 2018, 4 of them come from Norway. They have, in total, won 14 medals, which were nearly 40% of the total number of medals the country has won.

(Data source: Statista)

If we look into the history, we will draw similar conclusions. Norway has top 3 athletes in the winter Olympics history and they have contributed 40 medals in total, which is about 10% of the total number of medals the country has won since 1924.

(Data source: Statista)

To conclude, richer and more developed countries are more likely to have better performance in the winter Olympics. Multiple-medal-winning athletes are also part of the secret behind top countries' success. However, national power, evaluated by GDP and population, is not very important to a country's success, nor is income inequality.
